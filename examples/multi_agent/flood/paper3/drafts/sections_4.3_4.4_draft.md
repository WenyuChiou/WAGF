### 4.3 RQ2: Institutional Endogenization and Adaptation Equity

**Endogenizing government subsidy and insurance pricing has little aggregate effect on adaptation rates, but it changes where affordability constraints bind.** Comparing the Full model to the Flat-policy ablation, owner action distributions are statistically indistinguishable ($\chi^2$ = 2.549, $p$ = 0.466, Cramer's $V$ = 0.0128), and the renter difference is statistically detectable but substantively negligible ($\chi^2$ = 6.853, $p$ = 0.033, Cramer's $V$ = 0.021). This is the central aggregate result for RQ2: endogenous institutions do not materially shift overall adoption within the 13-year window. The yearly policy paths do move. The government subsidy rises from 0.52 in 2011 to a mid-period plateau near 0.68 before ending at 0.65, while the Community Rating System discount fluctuates near 0.18-0.20 and then falls to 0.00 in 2023. The interpretive point is therefore not that institutional agents broadly raise protection, but that endogenous policy mostly redistributes which households can convert protective intent into executed action under binding cost constraints (Bubeck et al., 2012; Rufat et al., 2015).

**The clearest RQ2 pattern appears in executed owner outcomes by marginalization status.** Under the Full model, marginalized (MG) owners execute insurance at 31.7%, elevation at 0.9%, and buyout at 0.2%, while 67.2% end in do-nothing. Non-marginalized (NMG) owners in the same runs execute insurance at 40.7%, elevation at 2.7%, and buyout at 1.6%, with 55.0% ending in do-nothing. The same hierarchy appears under Flat policies: MG owners remain at 32.2% insurance, 0.9% elevation, and 0.3% buyout, versus 42.6%, 2.9%, and 1.4% for NMG owners, with do-nothing at 66.7% for MG and 53.2% for NMG. These executed rates indicate that the model's main institutional sensitivity is distributive rather than aggregate. Adaptation inequality persists even when overall adoption is stable, which is consistent with flood risk research showing that low-income households face larger practical barriers to structural protection and program uptake even under shared hazard exposure (Grothmann & Reusswig, 2006; Rufat et al., 2015).

**Affordability blocking is concentrated among MG owners, and endogenous policy reduces that blocking relative to fixed assumptions.** In the Full model, MG owners experience 100 affordability rejections, or 2.56% of all MG owner decisions, compared with zero affordability rejections for NMG owners. Under Flat policies, MG owner affordability rejections rise to 247, or 6.33%, while NMG owners still face only 19 such rejections, or 0.49%. The institutional ablation therefore does not produce a large shift in final adoption shares, but it more than doubles the rate at which MG owner protective attempts are blocked on affordability grounds. This is the strongest equity signal in RQ2. It suggests that adaptive policy mainly operates by softening a cost filter that falls disproportionately on already vulnerable households, which matches the environmental justice literature on uneven access to mitigation resources (Rufat et al., 2015).

**The widening inequality appears most clearly when comparing attempted and executed owner decisions.** MG owners finish in do-nothing at 67.2% of household-years, compared with 55.0% for NMG owners, a 12.2 percentage point executed gap. At the proposal stage, the same gap is 8.3 percentage points, so the difference widens after validators apply feasibility and affordability constraints. MG owners also show a larger proposal-to-execution increase in do-nothing (+22.6 percentage points) than NMG owners (+18.8 percentage points). The key result is that the LLM does not generate a large equity split in intended protective choices on its own; the larger split appears when attempted actions meet the same financial and feasibility rules that households face in the simulated institutional setting. This pattern is consistent with the adaptation deficit framework: households may recognize risk and propose action, yet still end in inaction once response costs and implementation barriers become binding (Bubeck et al., 2012).

### 4.4 RQ3: Theory-Consistent Relationships Among Non-Enforced Psychological Constructs

**Among constructs that are not directly enforced by governance rules, the clearest theory-consistent signal is that higher stakeholder perception aligns with more protective action.** Stakeholder perception (SP) has no blocking rules in the governance pipeline, so its association with action is not circular. Across household decisions, agents that execute insurance report higher SP than agents that execute do-nothing (mean 2.75 versus 2.45; Mann-Whitney $U$ = 30,135,406, $p$ = 2.75 $\times 10^{-279}$). The action-level profiles show the same ordering: SP averages 2.80 for contents insurance, 2.75 for owner insurance, and 2.90 for renter relocation, compared with 2.45 for do-nothing. This pattern is consistent with flood adaptation theory in which confidence in institutions and relevant stakeholders supports uptake of protective measures, especially insurance products that depend on trust in outside actors and program legitimacy (Grothmann & Reusswig, 2006; Bubeck et al., 2012).

**Place attachment shows the expected relationship with mobility-oriented adaptation, especially for renters.** Place attachment (PA) is only minimally constrained in the model: high PA blocks owner buyout, but it does not govern renter relocation. Among renters, agents that execute relocation report lower PA than those that execute do-nothing (mean 2.61 versus 2.76; Mann-Whitney $U$ = 587,342.5, $p$ = 1.75 $\times 10^{-5}$). The action profiles reinforce this ordering. Relocation (PA = 2.61) and contents insurance (PA = 2.62) have the lowest average PA across actions, whereas owner insurance (PA = 2.94) and buyout (PA = 3.05) are higher. The renter result is the cleanest because it is not validator-enforced. It is consistent with the flood retreat literature showing that mobility becomes more plausible when attachment to home and place is weaker, even under repeated hazard exposure (Mach et al., 2019).

**Group differences in SP and PA also align with the model's executed adaptation inequalities.** MG households report lower SP than NMG households (2.57 versus 2.65; $p$ = 5.17 $\times 10^{-17}$) and higher PA (2.88 versus 2.76; $p$ = 1.24 $\times 10^{-30}$). The most interpretable four-cell pattern is MG owners, who combine the lowest SP (2.55) with the highest PA (3.01). This is the same group that records the highest executed do-nothing rate in RQ2. The relationship should be interpreted cautiously, but it is consistent with a trapped adaptation profile in which weaker confidence in institutions and stronger attachment to place coincide with lower realized uptake of disruptive or resource-intensive protective actions. That reading fits prior findings that social vulnerability, distrust, and attachment can jointly hinder formal adaptation despite repeated exposure (Rufat et al., 2015; Mach et al., 2019).

**Temporal change in these non-enforced constructs is present but weak, so the main RQ3 evidence is cross-sectional rather than dynamic.** SP increases slightly over the simulation period (Spearman $\rho$ = 0.0969, $p$ = 8.43 $\times 10^{-34}$), while PA declines slightly ($\rho$ = -0.0728, $p$ = 8.97 $\times 10^{-20}$). With $n$ = 15,584 decisions, these coefficients are statistically significant but small. The stronger descriptive pattern is exposure-linked rather than calendar-linked: SP rises with cumulative flood count ($\rho$ = 0.4576), whereas PA changes much less ($\rho$ = 0.0609). For RQ3, the relevant result is therefore not a strong temporal drift in psychology, but that unconstrained constructs retain interpretable relationships to executed behavior even after the governance system has filtered feasible actions. This supports the claim that the LLM-ABM produces some theory-consistent construct-behavior structure outside the rules that are explicitly enforced by the model (Rogers, 1983; Grothmann & Reusswig, 2006).
