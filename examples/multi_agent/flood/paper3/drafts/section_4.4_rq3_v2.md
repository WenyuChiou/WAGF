### 4.4 RQ3: Psychological Constructs and Deliberative Reasoning

**Before analyzing construct-behavior relationships, we verify that LLM-reported constructs have semantic grounding in the reasoning text.** Stakeholder perception (SP) and place attachment (PA) correspond to the language agents use when justifying choices rather than acting as arbitrary self-reports. Agents with low SP use distrust-oriented language in 40.7% of decisions, whereas high-SP agents do so in only 22.2%, a 1.8x gradient. This pattern is not driven by simple mention frequency: institutional references appear in roughly 85% of decisions across all SP levels, so SP captures sentiment polarity toward institutions rather than whether they are mentioned at all. Place attachment shows a comparable pattern. High-PA agents use attachment-related language in 85.9% of decisions, compared with 66.6% for low-PA agents. High-PA agents also mention relocation in 59.2% of cases, but usually as an option considered and rejected because of social ties, neighborhood familiarity, or disruption. These gradients indicate that the construct labels are semantically grounded and interpretable as meaningful psychological signals.

**Among constructs not enforced by governance rules, SP and PA show strong associations with adaptation behavior.** This distinction matters because SP has no blocking rules anywhere in the governance pipeline, and PA only constrains owner buyout at high levels; it does not govern renter relocation. Agents choosing insurance report higher SP than agents choosing do-nothing (mean SP = 2.75 versus 2.45; Mann-Whitney p = 2.75 x 10^-279; n = 13,700). The action profile shows the same gradient at the categorical level: 68.0% of high-SP decisions result in protective action, compared with 32.0% for low-SP decisions. This pattern is consistent with research showing that trust in institutions supports uptake of formally mediated protective measures such as insurance (Terpstra, 2011). PA shows the reverse pattern for renter mobility. Renters who relocate report lower place attachment than renters who choose do-nothing (mean PA = 2.61 versus 2.76; Mann-Whitney p = 1.75 x 10^-5; n = 4,959), consistent with evidence that stronger place bonds reduce willingness to leave exposed locations (De Dominicis et al., 2015). The cross-sectional evidence therefore supports theory-consistent construct-behavior alignment for the least governance-contaminated constructs.

**Construct changes within individual agents accompany behavioral transitions, suggesting that these measures are not merely post-hoc labels attached to fixed choices.** Among agents whose SP increased between adjacent years, 34.5% switched from do-nothing to a protective action. Among agents whose SP remained stable, only 19.6% made the same transition (chi-squared = 128.86, p < 10^-6). The pattern is monotonic: agents with increasing SP show 56.1% protective action, SP-stable agents show 47.8%, and agents with declining SP show 42.3%. A lagged analysis points in the same direction, with SP at year t predicting protective action at year t+1 (chi-squared = 140, p < 10^-4). These results should not be read as proof of causation, but they do show that shifts in stakeholder perception and shifts in action co-occur within the same decision histories. That goes beyond simple cross-sectional correlation because it tracks behavioral change inside the same agent over time.

**Despite the construct-behavior associations, LLM agents can override construct-implied actions through deliberative reasoning, which means constructs act as inputs to judgment rather than deterministic action triggers.** The clearest case is deliberative inaction under high motivation. Among decisions with both high threat perception and high stakeholder perception, 31.0% (122/396) still chose do-nothing. These cases were approved by validators rather than blocked, and the reasoning repeatedly cites cost burden, cumulative exhaustion, and rational prioritization of other household needs. The agents recognize danger and do not express distrust toward institutions, yet still conclude that action is not worth the burden at that moment. The reverse pattern also appears. Among low-SP decisions, 23.5% (1,525/6,492) still purchased insurance. Here the reasoning often frames insurance as a pragmatic hedge because flood losses would exceed the premium, even when the agent remains skeptical of institutions. Both patterns are consistent with an override mechanism in which deliberation tempers what the construct profile alone would imply. The reasoning traces therefore suggest that psychological constructs shape the decision field, while cost-benefit appraisal, fatigue, and feasibility concerns can redirect the final choice.

**The construct-behavior relationships converge to explain why marginalized homeowners show the highest inaction rates in the model.** This group combines the lowest stakeholder perception (SP = 2.55) with the highest place attachment (PA = 3.01) among the four tenure-by-marginalization cells. The same group also records the highest executed do-nothing rate in RQ2 at 67.2%. Lower SP is associated with weaker insurance uptake, while higher PA is associated with lower willingness to leave place when disruption becomes severe. Those psychological tendencies then meet the affordability filters documented in RQ2, where income-gated validators sharply limit the execution of costly owner actions such as elevation. The result is a trapped profile defined by three pressures operating together: low institutional trust, strong attachment to place, and structural barriers to expensive protection. RQ3 therefore suggests that the highest inaction rates emerge where multiple psychological frictions align with binding feasibility constraints, especially for marginalized owners facing repeated flood exposure.
