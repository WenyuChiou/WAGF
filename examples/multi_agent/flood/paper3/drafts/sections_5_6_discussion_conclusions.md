## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What LLM-ABM Adds to Flood Adaptation Modeling

**The main contribution of the LLM-ABM is methodological rather than substitutive.** Relative to a traditional Bayesian ABM, the present framework adds auditable reasoning traces, explicit construct-action deliberation, and a visible gap between proposed and executed behavior that reveals where institutional constraints bind. Those additions matter because many flood adaptation choices are shaped by affordability, disruption, and feasibility barriers that remain latent inside Bernoulli trial outputs even when aggregate rates are well calibrated (Grothmann & Reusswig, 2006; Bubeck et al., 2012). The reasoning traces show that households often recognize flood risk yet still end in do-nothing because protection is judged unaffordable or not worth the disruption. The validator layer then makes those constraints inspectable rather than implicit. This should not be read as an argument that LLM-ABM replaces traditional ABM. Traditional ABM remains useful for large-scale calibration, while LLM-ABM functions as a methodological lens for inspecting how narrative judgment, institutional filtering, and behavioral frictions shape adaptation trajectories.

### 5.2 Governance Operates Through Two Channels

**The governance framework appears to operate through both enforcement and framing, with their relative importance likely depending on model capacity.** The first channel is enforcement: validators block actions that violate affordability, feasibility, or construct-action coherence, and this channel is especially important for a weak model such as Gemma 3 4B because it prevents implausible outputs from becoming executed behavior. The second channel is framing: the prompt structure itself, including the default-override architecture and the ordering of institutional context, shapes what the model treats as reasonable before any validator intervenes. The RQ2 results show that validators matter most for equity because they turn similar proposed MG and NMG owner choices into materially different executed outcomes. Prompt framing also reduces unrealistic action bias by foregrounding hassle costs, local barriers, and inaction as the default option. Preliminary single-seed single-agent flood tests with Gemma 4 suggest that stronger models may require less hard blocking to maintain plausibility, but that evidence remains limited and should be treated cautiously. ### 5.3 Limitations: Model Artifacts

**Several counterintuitive results should be interpreted as model artifacts or measurement limitations rather than behavioral findings.** The clearest example is the coping perception reversal: agents labeled CP=H were more passive than CP=M agents, which is opposite to PMT expectations. The most plausible interpretation is semantic misreading by Gemma 3 4B, where "high coping" is understood as being able to live with current conditions rather than being capable of undertaking costly protective action. This reading is consistent with the rarity of CP=H responses and with the absence of the same pattern in preliminary Gemma 4 checks, so the issue is better treated as a scale artifact than as a challenge to PMT itself (Rogers, 1983; Grothmann & Reusswig, 2006). Threat perception also shows strong year-to-year persistence, with about 84% stability versus roughly 38% expected under an even five-category redistribution, but that persistence likely mixes memory continuity with category anchoring. A related artifact appears in the weak socioeconomic differentiation of proposed actions, where the equity gap emerges mainly after validators impose binding constraints.

### 5.4 Limitations: Design Choices

**The current design also limits what can be inferred about mechanism and generalizability.** Most importantly, there is no memory ablation, so memory-rich reasoning cannot be isolated from the broader decision context and should be discussed only as consistent with persistence rather than as a demonstrated driver. The social channels are also weak in their current form; gossip citations are negatively associated with protection (RR = 0.75), which suggests descriptive exposure to inaction norms more than productive social learning. Reproducibility is acceptable at three seeds, but a larger seed pool would provide a firmer basis for subgroup interpretation, especially for rare actions. The study is also confined to the Passaic River Basin, so transferability to other hydrologic, institutional, or housing contexts remains unresolved (Grimm et al., 2005; Oreskes et al., 1994).

### 5.5 Implications for Practice

**For applied flood modeling, the governance framework is most useful as a diagnostic tool for identifying where institutions alter realized adaptation.** Running the same household decision model with and without validators can quantify how much apparent self-regulation is actually produced by institutional feasibility gates. That diagnostic use is especially important for equity analysis because the model shows that marginalized owners are not distinguished mainly by lower stated intent, but by lower conversion of intent into executed action once affordability and disruption are enforced. The MG-owner profile, combining the lowest stakeholder perception, the highest place attachment, and the highest executed do-nothing rate, points to the need for targeted interventions at the intersection of distrust, immobility, and financial constraint rather than generic risk communication alone (Rufat et al., 2015; Mach et al., 2019). The practical implication is that institutional endogenization matters less for aggregate adoption totals than for who is filtered out of protective pathways.

### 5.6 Future Directions

**The next step is to test whether the need for governance declines as model capability increases, while improving measurement of continuous behavioral variation.** A cross-model scaling analysis should compare small and larger models under identical prompts and validators to determine when governance acts as an essential safety net and when it becomes mostly redundant. Construct measurement should move from coarse five-point bins to seven-point or continuous sliders so that real stability can be separated more cleanly from anchoring. A dedicated memory ablation is needed to distinguish narrative persistence from simple state carryover. Additional extensions include multi-community spatial interaction and real-time policy feedback integration so that government and insurance agents respond to more than annual summary statistics. Those steps would preserve the framework's value as an auditable modeling lens while making its behavioral diagnostics more portable across water resource domains.

## 6. Conclusions

**RQ1 shows that the LLM-ABM converges with the traditional ABM on tightly constrained actions but diverges on high-frequency behaviors, and the reasoning traces make those divergences interpretable in terms of adaptation deficits and intention-action gaps.** **RQ2 shows that endogenous government and insurance decisions have little aggregate effect within the study window, yet they materially change adaptation equity by determining where affordability constraints block execution, especially for marginalized owners.** **RQ3 shows that non-enforced constructs, especially stakeholder perception and place attachment, retain theory-consistent relationships with executed behavior, while deliberative overrides keep those constructs from becoming mechanical action rules.** Taken together, these findings position the LLM-ABM as a methodological lens that complements rather than replaces traditional ABM by adding auditable reasoning, explicit construct-action deliberation, and institutional constraint diagnostics. The results also suggest that governance functions as a capability-dependent safety net: it is essential for weaker models such as Gemma 3 4B, and preliminary evidence indicates it may become less necessary as model capacity improves, though that claim requires broader testing. The framework is being prepared as an open-source research artifact for other flood and water resources applications.
