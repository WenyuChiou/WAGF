By preserving the audit trail of proposal, rejection, revision, and execution, WAGF operationalises a claim Ostrom (1990) made about institutions: rules do not merely constrain behaviour but enable coordinated action by structuring feasible choices. Across the irrigation experiment, validators reduced IBR from 61.1% to 38.2%, lowered the demand ratio from 0.770 to 0.411, and kept Lake Mead above 988 ft rather than allowing collapse to the 895 ft dead-pool threshold. In the flood experiment, validators reduced IBR from 8.0% to 0.8% while keeping EHE in a similar range (0.861 with validators versus 0.815 without validators). These results show that WAGF's value is not different actions for their own sake, but making the link between appraisal, attempted action, and institutional rejection observable. WAGF extends that idea into water agent-based modelling by giving water researchers a computational way to observe how rules shape the translation from scarcity appraisal to decision.

The Lake Mead result requires a dual interpretation rather than a simple conservation reading. With validators, agents did not become uniformly cautious. They still extracted more water per allowed unit than a conservative rule-based baseline, yet they maintained a higher minimum Mead elevation through staggered timing and responsive curtailment. Without validators, by contrast, higher extraction was concentrated in repeated demand increases that persisted even under high shortage appraisal, pushing the reservoir to dead pool. The contrast suggests that rules did not merely suppress use; they changed when and under what conditions extraction occurred. This is why the validators are better understood as a behavioural guardrail than as a conservation switch. They prevented agents from acting on the most damaging proposals while still leaving room for adaptive exploitation within the feasible range. That reading fits the v12 results more closely than any claim that governance simply means lower demand, and it matters for water policy because it shows that institutional performance can involve better timing rather than less use in every period.

The strongest new policy signal appears at the boundary between Water Shortage Appraisal and Adaptive Capacity Appraisal. In the high-scarcity, high-capacity quadrant, 51.2% of proposals with validators still attempted demand increases, and validators rejected 56.3% of all proposals in that quadrant. Without validators, the comparable increase rate was 71.1%. This is not random residual noise. It is a structured form of attempted non-compliance concentrated where agents see both threat and room to maneuver. That pattern resembles anticipatory extraction in western prior-appropriation systems, where users may divert earlier or more aggressively when they expect later curtailment, a pattern discussed by Libecap (2011). The value of WAGF is that it turns this boundary behaviour into an observable object rather than an inferred one. Conventional water agent-based models can record executed diversion, but they do not preserve the attempted proposal that an institution blocks. Here the blocked proposal becomes evidence about where institutional pressure is strongest and where future empirical work should look for pre-emptive response.

The cross-hazard comparison indicates that this mechanism is not limited to one task or one model family. The same architecture reduced irrational behaviour in chronic irrigation scarcity and in single-agent flood adaptation, even though the flood domain offered only four actions: insurance, elevation, relocation, and do_nothing. Across nine paired flood-model comparisons, IBR was non-increasing with validators in every pair, and seven of nine reductions reached p < 0.05. EHE was largely preserved, with only small shifts in most models, which supports the claim that validators reduce irrational behaviour without forcing the same behavioural mix in every run. The irrigation findings above rely on a single model family (Gemma-3 4B, five seeds); cross-model evidence established here is flood-domain only. Whether the irrigation institutions-as-enablers pattern persists across model scales is a testable prediction for future work. This matters because it separates the framework claim from any single-model result. The evidence spans Gemma 3 (4B, 12B, 27B), Gemma 4 (e2b, e4b, 26B), and Ministral (3B, 8B, 14B) — a three-family grid covering two to twenty-seven billion parameters. At the largest Gemma-4 scale the validators not only reduced IBR but produced a small EHE gain (+0.034), consistent with institutions-as-enablers rather than institutions-as-constraints at model scales where the base model already has sufficient capacity to represent diverse adaptive options. In that sense, WAGF should be read as a transferable water-governance instrument whose pattern holds across hazard type and model scale, while being reported with the limits of the current dataset.

The governance architecture extends naturally from point-in-time decision rules to sequence-level trajectory rules. We define three domain-agnostic temporal rule classes — M1 appraisal–history coherence, M2 behavioural inertia, and M3 evidence-grounded irreversibility — and evaluate them post-hoc across the nine flood-model configurations (Supplementary Note 11, Supplementary Table 7). M1 is of particular interest: for smaller Gemma-4 variants, threat appraisal sometimes reverts to the low set within one or two simulated years of a salient flood event despite the event remaining in agent memory. This pattern is invisible to point-in-time validators (which check only the current-year appraisal–action pair) but detectable by M1. The diagnostic result identifies a concrete mechanism gap: runtime validators establish appraisal–action coherence at each decision, but temporal coherence across decisions is not enforced. We also find that the act of validator interception produces two distinct agent compliance strategies — majority action upgrade (threat maintained, action upgraded to match) and minority appraisal downgrade (action retained, threat relabelled lower; Supplementary Table 8). The action-upgrade majority is consistent with the canonical PMT response of matching protective intensity to perceived threat; the appraisal-downgrade minority resembles motivated reappraisal (Festinger 1957). Live enforcement of temporal rules via the reflection engine's year-end hook, rather than via pre-execution block-and-retry, is the architecturally natural extension and is reserved for follow-up work; the present paper establishes the rule formalism and diagnostic signal.

Several limitations frame what WAGF currently establishes. The irrigation analysis is a single-model experiment, and the flood side is limited to the four-action single-agent setting rather than a broader household market or community adaptation system. The Lake Mead model is not a calibrated Colorado River forecasting tool, so the present results should be read as evidence about institutional dynamics, not as direct prediction of observed basin operations. The study also does not yet validate reasoning traces against real irrigator or household evidence. Comparing blocked and revised traces against interview-based studies, compliance records, or observed responses to shortage announcements would provide a stronger basis for causal interpretation.

A distinct methodological caveat concerns prompt design. The reported validator effect is contingent on our specific personas, decision-framing templates, and memory configuration. A fundamental alternative hypothesis cannot be ruled out by a single-prompt experiment: that sufficiently careful prompt engineering could induce scarcity-consistent behaviour without validators, making the rule layer redundant rather than enabling. Establishing the validator effect as irreducible to prompt design requires a systematic prompt-sensitivity study. A concrete design would hold the validator stack fixed and vary prompt components across a factorial grid — persona framing (first-person vs. institutional role), construct-reasoning format (free-text vs. structured appraisal), memory scope (episodic vs. cumulative), and decision vocabulary (discrete vs. magnitude) — with five seeds per cell. The predicted WAGF outcome is that without-validator IBR remains elevated across all prompt configurations while with-validators IBR stays close to floor. The predicted null outcome is that some prompt configuration erases the gap. Running this test is the most direct way to discriminate between institutions-as-enablers and prompts-as-substitute framings and is the next experiment we would prioritise.

Bankes (1993) argued that exploratory models are most useful when they clarify how assumptions shape possible futures. WAGF adds to that tradition by making attempted but blocked water decisions visible. Hung and Yang (2021) showed how water agents can encode institutional logic numerically; this study shows that language-based agents can expose where that logic is resisted, revised, and finally accepted.
