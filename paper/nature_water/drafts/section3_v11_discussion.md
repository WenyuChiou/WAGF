## Discussion

The central finding is that institutional rules function as cognitive infrastructure for water-resource reasoning. Without rules defining shortage tiers, demand ceilings, and curtailment triggers, LLM agents cannot organize conservation reasoning (IBR 91% versus 42%). This addresses the question of whether governance merely constrains behaviour or creates conditions for adaptive water management. The answer parallels Ostrom's (1990) observation for human communities: well-designed institutions do not merely restrict. They define feasibility boundaries within which diverse adaptive strategies become viable. We emphasize that the parallel is structural. Ostrom's analysis involves strategic interaction and norm internalization, whereas our governance rules evaluate each proposal independently without memory of previous decisions. Whether informal norms emerge from repeated agent interaction remains untested. We use "reasoning" in a functional sense to describe the structured text generation process by which LLM agents produce decisions, without implying human-like cognition.

The structured non-compliance revealed by appraisal decomposition offers a diagnostic capacity absent from conventional agent-based water models. The 42% governed IBR is not random noise; it concentrates in agents reporting both high water-shortage appraisal and high adaptive capacity, a pattern consistent with anticipatory extraction ("use it or lose it") observed in real drought-responsive allocation systems where irrigators historically increase diversions in anticipation of curtailment. Because the governance process records every blocked proposal alongside the agent's reasoning, it creates an audit trail of decisions that agents attempted but the institution prevented. This generates structured hypotheses about how institutional boundaries shape water-use reasoning, hypotheses amenable to empirical testing with observed irrigator data. Conventional models cannot produce this diagnostic because agents execute programmed allocation rules and cannot generate proposals that violate those rules.

The demand ceiling ablation reveals the mechanism linking institutional design to behavioural coherence. Removing this single rule, which connects individual demand proposals to aggregate basin state, increased diversity but raised IBR and nearly doubled shortage frequency. This separates two properties that institutional theory has long distinguished but seldom quantified computationally: diversity (wider repertoire of actions used) and adaptive diversity (actions consistent with agents' own scarcity assessments). The same approach could test variations in shortage-sharing thresholds, allocation regimes, insurance premium structures, or drought contingency triggers, observing how reasoning agents adapt within physically valid bounds. This capacity is distinct from exploratory modelling (Bankes, 1993) and sensitivity analysis in that the behavioural responses are generated, not sampled from predefined parameter spaces.

Our governance architecture suppresses anticipatory-extraction proposals; future designs could distinguish between physically infeasible allocations and institutionally prohibited-but-observed diversion strategies, preserving the latter as diagnostic windows into water-use reasoning under scarcity. This distinguishes boundary constraints (what agents cannot do) from prescriptive constraints (what agents should do). The majority of our rules enforce the former, leaving strategy selection to the agent.

Several scope conditions apply. Governance did not significantly alter behavioural diversity in any of the six models tested (ΔEHE range: −0.063 to +0.021; all 95% CIs include zero). The IBR reduction, however, was strongest for smaller models with higher baseline violation rates (Gemma-3 4B: 8.1% to 0.6%; Ministral 3B: 5.0% to 0.3%) and weakest for models with near-zero baseline IBR (Gemma-3 12B: 0.2% to 0.1%). This pattern suggests that governance provides the greatest benefit where model-intrinsic alignment with behavioural theory is weakest. Only one model (Gemma-3 4B) was tested in the irrigation domain; the multi-scale analysis is limited to flood. The reservoir model simplifies the full CRSS system to a single reservoir; results should be interpreted as stylized institutional dynamics. Each experimental condition was replicated with three independent seeds, consistent with standard practice in agent-based water modelling but limiting formal inferential power.

We note that doubling the insurance premium rate reversed the governance diversity effect in the flood domain (Supplementary Note 5), indicating that adaptive diversity depends on the institutional parameter configuration and is not an unconditional property of governance. The two domains also showed contrasting non-compliance structures: persistent boundary-testing in irrigation versus rapid internalization with cognitive compression in flood. This suggests that the qualitative character of governance effects may depend on the decision context.

Despite these limitations, the governance effect is consistent across models: significant IBR reduction in four of six, non-significant diversity change in all six, structured non-compliance in both domains, and experimental decomposability of individual rules. This consistency suggests that institutional rules are a robust mechanism for constraining irrational behaviour in LLM-based water simulation without sacrificing adaptive diversity. The question for water research shifts from whether LLM agents can represent human water reasoning to what institutional designs best govern that reasoning, and what those designs reveal about how institutions structure the cognitive space of water decisions.
