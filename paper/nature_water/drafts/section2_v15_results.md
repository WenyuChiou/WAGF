## Results

### Without institutional rules, agents fail to act on their own scarcity assessments

Two experimental conditions isolate the governance effect on water-resource reasoning (Fig. 2a, Extended Data Table 1; all irrigation results use Gemma-3 4B, 78 agents ? 42 years, 5 runs each). With-validators agents (left) maintained demand dominated by maintain actions throughout the 42-year horizon, with Lake Mead remaining above 988 ft. LLM (no validator) agents (right) shifted progressively toward demand increases, depleting Lake Mead to dead-pool elevation (895 ft) in every run. System-level outcomes reveal the consequence but not the mechanism. To identify where reasoning fails, we measure the Irrational Behaviour Rate (IBR): the fraction of decisions where an agent reports high water-shortage appraisal yet still proposes a demand increase. LLM (no validator) agents exhibited IBR of 61.1%, compared to 38.2% for with-validators agents, a 22.9-percentage-point reduction. The no-validator IBR exceeds the null expectation under uniform random action selection (40%, since 2 of 5 actions are increases), indicating a directional bias toward demand increases that governance reduces.

Without governance, agents extracted nearly twice as much water (demand ratio 0.770 versus 0.411). LLM (no validator) agents triggered shortage in 39 of 42 years, whereas with-validators agents experienced shortage in 14 of 42 years (Extended Data Table 1). LLM (no validator) agents could not translate their own scarcity assessments into conservation behaviour, extracting aggressively even as the reservoir they depend on approached failure. The demand-ratio gap does not tell the full story. With validators, agents still extracted more water per allowed unit than a conservative rule-based baseline (Supplementary Note 8), yet the system-level outcome was a higher minimum Mead elevation (988 ft versus 895 ft dead pool). The contrast indicates that validators reshape when and under what conditions extraction occurs — staggered across years and responsive to curtailment signals — rather than simply lowering use in every period.

LLM (no validator) agents produced higher behavioural diversity than with-validators agents (Effective Heterogeneity Entropy, EHE, 0.848 versus 0.687; see Methods). Prompt design (persona, decision framing, memory template) affects both absolute IBR and EHE levels; the with-versus-without-validators contrast isolates the validator effect while holding prompt design constant within each run. We distinguish adaptive diversity, where action distributions vary systematically with agents' self-assessed scarcity, from arbitrary diversity, where actions are distributed broadly but independently of scarcity appraisal. The higher no-validator EHE is arbitrary: 61.1% of high-scarcity decisions still proposed demand increases, indicating that the wider action distribution reflects extraction uncoupled from scarcity assessment.

In the flood domain, governance produced a complementary pattern (Extended Data Table 2): irrational behaviour decreased tenfold (IBR 8.0% to 0.8%) while behavioural diversity showed no significant difference (EHE 0.861 with validators versus 0.815 without, p = 0.166). In irrigation, governance constrains over-extraction and lowers diversity from an arbitrarily high baseline; in flood, governance constrains irrational inaction while preserving an already-adaptive diversity level.

### Non-compliance concentrates at the boundary of high scarcity and high adaptive capacity

The 38% with-validators IBR is not randomly distributed across the agent population. Decomposing decisions by agents' self-assessed Water Shortage Appraisal (WSA) and Adaptive Capacity Appraisal (ACA) reveals that non-compliance concentrates at a specific institutional boundary: the intersection of high scarcity awareness and high adaptive capacity.

In the high-scarcity quadrant (WSA = H or VH, ACA = H or VH), 51.2% of with-validators decisions proposed demand increases. Governance rejected 56.3% of all proposals in this quadrant, including every demand-increase proposal. The high-threat/high-capacity rule triggered on 72.2% of decisions in this quadrant (Fig. 2b). LLM (no validator) agents in the same quadrant proposed increases at 71.1%. The governance effect on increase proposals was largest (32.5 percentage points) at moderate scarcity, with reductions of 19-28 percentage points at other WSA levels.

This concentration reveals a patterned reasoning failure. Agents that perceive severe scarcity *and* assess themselves as highly capable are precisely those most likely to attempt demand increases. This reasoning pattern is consistent with anticipatory extraction ("use it or lose it") documented in western US prior-appropriation systems (Libecap, 2011). The governance rules intercept these proposals before execution, and the natural-language reasoning traces record why agents attempted them. During Tier 3 shortage, for example, an agent reasoned: "my previous aggressive increase failed... given my scepticism of forecasts, I will cautiously increase," acknowledging failure while repeating it (Supplementary Note 6). Critically, the boundary pattern is not reducible to exploration: 51.2 % of with-validators proposals at the high-scarcity, high-capacity quadrant are demand increases, yet at the same quadrant water shortage appraisal is self-rated H or VH. Exploration would not concentrate increases precisely where the agent has already diagnosed scarcity. This diagnostic capacity, observing what agents attempt at institutional boundaries rather than only what they execute, is not available in conventional agent-based water models where agents execute predetermined rules.

In the flood domain, validators produce a contrasting non-compliance structure (Fig. 3b). Rather than persistent boundary-testing, with-validators flood agents rapidly internalized Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)-consistent behaviour: only 0.8% of with-validators flood proposals were rejected by validators, compared to 40.2% of with-validators irrigation proposals. But this internalization came with a cost. Ninety-two percent of with-validators agents self-assessed coping appraisal at a single level (M, moderate; Supplementary Table 5), suggesting that the validator effect in flood may partially reflect compression of cognitive differentiation rather than genuinely diverse adaptive reasoning. The two domains thus reveal complementary institutional dynamics: in irrigation, validators create a hard boundary that agents persistently test, while in flood, validators produce rapid compliance but narrower cognitive variation (Extended Data Table 2).

### The governance effect generalizes across water hazard types and model scales

The governance mechanism that constrains irrational behaviour in chronic drought generalizes to acute flood hazard, though the domain-specific pattern differs (Fig. 3a). In the flood domain, 100 household agents made protective decisions (insurance, elevation, relocation, or inaction) over 10 years with stochastic flood events. Governed LLM agents (left) and LLM (no validator) agents (right) both developed diversified protection portfolios, whereas rule-based PMT agents (centre) converged on insurance with near-zero relocation. Governed LLM agents produced higher behavioural diversity than these hand-coded agents (EHE 0.861 versus 0.598), despite both approaches using the same behavioural theory (Extended Data Table 2). A rule-based agent in the irrigation domain achieved comparable demand ratios but with 84-89% of decisions rejected by governance (Supplementary Note 8).

These irrigation results use a single model family (Gemma-3 4B, five seeds). Domain transferability was tested separately in the flood domain, where nine model configurations provide cross-scale evidence.
Nine models spanning three families and scales from 3B to 27B parameters supported the finding that validators reduce irrational behaviour while largely preserving adaptive diversity (Supplementary Fig. 1). IBR was non-increasing under validators across all nine pairs, and eight of nine reductions reached statistical significance (p < 0.05; Supplementary Table 6). Smaller models with higher baseline violation rates showed the largest reductions (Gemma-3 4B: 8.5% to 0.9%, ΔIBR = 7.7 pp, p < 0.001; Gemma-4 e4b: 7.9% to 0.5%, ΔIBR = 7.5 pp, p = 0.001). The largest Gemma-4 configuration showed the largest reduction in the nine-model grid (Gemma-4 26B: 9.3% to 0.1%, ΔIBR = 9.2 pp, p < 0.001). The only non-significant pair was Gemma-3 12B (0.3% to 0.0%, p = 0.056), a near-zero-baseline floor case. EHE changes under validators were small in magnitude in most models (|ΔEHE| ≤ 0.07); the Ministral family showed consistent small EHE reductions (−0.02 to −0.05), while Gemma-4 26B was the striking exception with a significant EHE gain under validators (+0.124, p = 0.008), consistent with validators enabling rather than constraining behavioural options at 26B parameter scale. Validators thus consistently eliminate irrational behaviour across model scales while largely preserving — and in one case expanding — adaptive diversity.

The consistency across two water domains and nine model configurations suggests that governance functions as a behavioural guardrail. Rules do not dictate which strategy agents choose but ensure that each strategy falls within physically and institutionally valid bounds. Domain-specific rule configurations replace the need to redesign decision logic for each application, addressing a longstanding limitation of agent-based water models.
