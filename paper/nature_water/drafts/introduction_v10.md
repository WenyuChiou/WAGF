## Introduction

For six decades, computational models of water resource systems have represented human decisions as mappings from system states to actions. The representation may take the form of a linear program, a coupled differential equation, or an agent's decision rule. In each case, the person deciding how much water to divert or whether to insure against flooding becomes a parameterized function. This approach, rooted in the Harvard Water Program (Maass et al., 1962) and codified in standard water systems analysis (Loucks and van Beek, 2017), enabled rigorous engineering but compressed the reasoning process into a numerical object. It left open a question central to water governance: not just what people decide, but how they reason toward those decisions.

Subsequent frameworks expanded the scope of human-water modelling without departing from this numerical format. Coupled human-natural systems theory established that human decisions and hydrological processes co-evolve through feedback loops (Liu et al., 2007). Sivapalan et al. (2012) proposed sociohydrology as a science of people and water, and subsequent work formalized levee effects, supply-demand cycles, and reservoir operation as coupled differential equations (Di Baldassarre et al., 2013; Di Baldassarre et al., 2015). However, human behaviour entered these equations as population-level variables rather than individual deliberative choices (Di Baldassarre et al., 2019). Two-way coupled agent-hydrology platforms made these interactions computationally explicit for individual agents (Lin and Yang, 2022), but the decision architecture remains numerical. As Blair and Buytaert (2016) observed, models could represent that populations respond to flood risk, but not how individuals reason toward protective action.

Agent-based modelling introduced individual-level decision-making by representing heterogeneous agents who act on local information and interact through shared environments (Epstein and Axtell, 1996; Bonabeau, 2002). In water research, early applications used rule-based or optimization-driven agents to simulate irrigation scheduling and decentralized watershed management (Berglund, 2015; Yang et al., 2009). Behavioural theory then entered these models: Haer et al. (2017) applied Protection Motivation Theory to household flood-adaptation choices, and Hyun and Yang (2019) integrated risk-perception updating into coupled agent-hydrology models. Hung and Yang (2021) developed a fuzzy Q-learning framework for drought-responsive Colorado River demand management, demonstrating how encoded institutional rules shape allocation outcomes. Hung and Yang (2022) further quantified adaptation uncertainty in coupled agent-reservoir systems under nonstationary climate. Other approaches introduced reinforcement learning for groundwater management (Castilla-Rho et al., 2017). However, all these approaches map numerical state vectors to numerical actions and require domain-specific redesign from dozens of behavioural theories (An, 2012; Müller et al., 2013; Schlüter et al., 2017). Each restricts agents to the same cognitive model with different parameter values.

The reasoning compressed by numerical formats may become accessible through a different decision format: agents that deliberate in natural language. A large language model (LLM)-based agent receives contextual information, including drought indices, water rights, flood-risk exposure, neighbour behaviour, and institutional announcements. It then proposes an action through natural-language reasoning that can reference domain knowledge, weigh trade-offs, and articulate justifications. LLM-based agents have demonstrated socially believable behaviours, including persona maintenance and adaptive planning, in multi-agent simulations (Park et al., 2023; Vezhnevets et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024). These results establish that natural language can represent the intermediate reasoning steps that numerical decision functions omit.

However, LLM agents without external structure produce two classes of failure. The first is physical: an agent may propose an allocation that violates mass balance or exceeds its water right. Governance rules can intercept these violations directly. The second is more subtle. An agent may correctly appraise drought as severe but still propose a demand increase, or perceive no flood threat but choose relocation, the costliest protective measure. These reasoning failures differ from factual hallucinations, cases where a model generates false information (Huang et al., 2025): the agent possesses the relevant knowledge but acts against its own assessment. Such appraisal-action inconsistencies parallel bounded rationality in human decision-makers, but natural-language traces make them directly observable. Their frequency varies across model families and scales (see Results). The question is not whether LLM agents can reason about water decisions, but whether their reasoning can be aligned with the physical and institutional rules that govern real water systems.

Here we develop the Water Agent Governance Framework (WAGF), which screens each agent's proposed behaviour against hydrological and policy constraints and returns explanatory feedback when proposals are rejected (Fig. 1). WAGF extends the demand-management model of Hung and Yang (2021) from numerical optimisation to natural-language reasoning and applies the same governed architecture to household flood adaptation. We show that validation rules constrain irrational behaviour without suppressing adaptive diversity: in the flood domain, validators reduce irrational behaviour tenfold (from 8.1% to 0.8%) while behavioural diversity remains statistically unchanged. With validators, irrigator agents reduce demand as reservoirs decline and household agents match protective investment to flood risk. Without validators, agents appraise the same hazard signals but fail to act on them. Chronic drought and acute flooding engage governance through fundamentally different pathways: irrigators persistently test allocation boundaries while flood-exposed households exhibit threat-dominated reasoning. These patterns hold across Colorado River irrigation and household flood adaptation, and across six LLMs spanning two model families and three parameter scales. Rule design, rather than model scale alone, determines whether LLM agents produce behaviourally realistic water-management decisions.
